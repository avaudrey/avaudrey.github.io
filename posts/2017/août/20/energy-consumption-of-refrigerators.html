Dynamic behaviour and energy consumption of refrigeration systems

Posted on dim. 20 août 2017 in engineering

What is the problem?

Let us suppose an amount, so a mass noted \(m\), of stuff (probably of food), we want to refrigerate, i.e. maintain at a temperature \(T_{\text{food}}\) lower than the ambient one noted \(T_0 : T_{\text{food}} \leq T_0\). From an engineering point of view, maintaining anything at a constant temperature does not really mean anything practical. We then rather want to maintain the stuff within a temperature range, possibly narrow, such as \(T_{\min} \leq T_{\text{food}} \leq T_{\max}\). For example, we want to maintain the inside of our refrigerator within a temperature range such as \(4°{\rm C} \leq T_{\text{food}} \leq 8°{\rm C}\) whereas the one in our kitchen is \(T_0=20°{\rm C}\).

The second law of thermodynamics being ruthless, whatever your refrigerator exactly is, if you wait for long enough, heat will “flow” from the hot to the cold, so from the outside of the refrigerator to its inside, with all the foreseeable detrimental consequences for your food as soon as \(T_{\text{food}} > T_{\max}\). To fight against this effect, your refrigerator will extract some heat, at a rate1 noted \(\dot{Q}_{\ominus}\) in watt, to it surroundings. For the sake of simplicity, we will suppose that the heat transfer rate \(\dot{Q}_{\ominus}\) extract by the refrigerator does not depend2 neither on the inside temperature \(T_{\text{food}}\), nor of the ambient one \(T_0\). The electrical power \(\dot{W}\) we will consume for such a purpose is linked to the refrigeration heat rate \(\dot{Q}_{\ominus}\) by the coefficient of performance of our refrigerator, noted \(\text{COP}\) and defined as:

$$ \text{COP} = \frac{\dot{Q}_{\ominus}}{\dot{W}}$$

and supposed as a constant as well. The operating mode of our refrigerator is thus really easy to grasp:

  • As long as \(T_{\min} \leq T_{\text{food}} \leq T_{\max}\), the refrigerator doesn’t work and, because of the heat coming from the outside, \(T_{\text{food}}\) increases.
  • Until \(T_{\text{food}} > T_{\max}\), and the refrigerator starts to extract the heat rate \(\dot{Q}_{\ominus}\), cooling down the stuff. Once \(T_{\text{food}} < T_{\min}\), the refrigerator stops, and so on.

Considering such an operating mode, the mass of stuff to refrigerate, and all the other parameters involved, what is the electricity consumption after a certain period? How to minimize such a consumption?
